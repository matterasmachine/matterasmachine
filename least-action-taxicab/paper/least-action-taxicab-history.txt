Least-Action-Like Path Selection from Discrete Exchange Histories
=================================================================

Abstract
--------

This note describes a possible microscopic interpretation of local
least-action-like path selection. The first layer is purely combinatorial: a
macroscopic path is treated as a coarse-grained class of microscopic histories,
and the effective action of that path is defined as the negative logarithm of
its probability or multiplicity. In regimes where the relevant classical
extremal is support-maximizing, the most probable macroscopic path is then the
path of least effective action.

The second layer gives a physical realization using a finite six-direction
instruction basis. A particle is represented by integer counts of momentum
quanta along the three pairs of opposite axial directions R, L, U, D, F, and B.
Interactions are local one-quantum add/remove exchanges. Silent replacement of
one quantum by another is not allowed. In this realization, velocity is the
normalized direction-count imbalance, momentum is the direction-count
imbalance itself, and force is the rate at which biased exchanges change that
imbalance.

The note does not claim to derive all of classical mechanics or all possible
fields. It derives a statistical action

    S_eff[path] = -log P[path],

then shows how the free Hamiltonian-like generator

    H_free(q) = q^2/(2N)

follows from the count-velocity relation in the simplest normalization. The
exact numerical coefficient of the multinomial counting penalty depends on the
chosen rest distribution and velocity normalization.

Finally, the note proves two bridge lemmas. The first converts local state
multiplicity into a path cost when coarse-grained history weights factorize in
time. The second gives a sufficient microscopic support law under which a
position-dependent exchange process produces the classical potential term with
the required sign. These are conditional theorems: their conclusions follow
from explicit hypotheses, but a concrete interaction must still be shown to
satisfy those hypotheses. The force/flux itself remains interaction-specific
and must be derived separately for each field.

The most important verification burdens are therefore visible from the start:
state multiplicity must become a per-time-slice path weight, and a concrete
field model must prove the potential-support law used for its position term.


1. The combinatorial layer
--------------------------

Consider a system with microscopic histories h and macroscopic paths P. A
macroscopic path is not one microscopic history. It is a coarse-grained class of
many microscopic histories:

    H(P) = { h : h is compatible with P }.

If all microscopic histories have equal base weight, then the probability of P
is proportional to its multiplicity:

    Prob(P) proportional to Omega(P),

where:

    Omega(P) = |H(P)|.

More generally, if microscopic histories have weights w(h), then:

    Prob(P) = sum_{h in H(P)} w(h).

Define the effective statistical action:

    S_eff(P) = -log Prob(P).

If histories are equally weighted, this becomes:

    S_eff(P) = -log Omega(P) + constant.

Therefore:

    most probable path = argmax Prob(P)
                       = argmin S_eff(P).

This is the minimal-action-like statement at the purely combinatorial level.
Nothing in this layer requires energy, force, mass, or even space. It only
requires microscopic histories, macroscopic coarse-graining, and path
probabilities.

This is not the textbook derivation of Hamilton's principle. It is the
statistical template behind a possible microscopic interpretation:

    action is the negative log support of a macroscopic path in microscopic
    history space.


2. Why this is not yet physics
------------------------------

The combinatorial layer alone does not know what a particle is. It does not
define:

    position,
    velocity,
    momentum,
    mass,
    energy,
    force,
    or potential.

Those quantities require a physical dictionary. The role of the discrete
direction realization below is to supply such a dictionary while keeping the
microscopic event rule discrete and countable.

The analogy with equilibrium statistical mechanics is useful. First one defines
entropy as a counting quantity:

    S_entropy = log Omega.

Only after energy and reservoirs are introduced does this become thermodynamics.
Similarly, here one first defines an action-like cost:

    S_eff = -log Prob(path).

Only after direction counts, momentum quanta, and exchange rules are introduced
does it become a candidate physical action.


3. Finite six-direction state space
-----------------------------------

The physical realization uses six primitive momentum directions:

    R, L, U, D, F, B.

These correspond to the positive and negative directions of three spatial axes.
A particle state is represented by six nonnegative integer counts:

    N_R, N_L, N_U, N_D, N_F, N_B.

The total count is:

    N = N_R + N_L + N_U + N_D + N_F + N_B.

If each quantum carries a fixed elementary energy epsilon, then:

    E = epsilon N.

Thus total energy accounting is total count accounting.

The directional imbalance is:

    q = (N_R - N_L, N_U - N_D, N_F - N_B).

The coarse velocity is defined as:

    v = q / N.

In this dictionary:

    total count N          = energy / inertial content,
    balanced directions    = rest-like content,
    directional imbalance  = momentum-like content,
    imbalance over count   = velocity.

This separates energy from motion. Two particles may have the same total count
N but different velocities because their direction counts have different
imbalances.

Rest mass is therefore not interpreted as the absence of momentum quanta. It is
interpreted as balanced momentum content. In one dimension, a rest-like state is:

    N_R = N/2
    N_L = N/2.

A moving state with the same total count is:

    N_R = N/2 + k
    N_L = N/2 - k.

The total count, and therefore the energy accounting in this simplified
picture, is unchanged. What changed is the imbalance:

    q = N_R - N_L = 2k.

This distinction will be used below to separate physical energy accounting from
statistical action cost.


4. Euclidean direction from discrete axial proportions
-------------------------------------------------------

For a requested unit Euclidean direction:

    u = (u_x, u_y, u_z),

with:

    u_x^2 + u_y^2 + u_z^2 = 1,

one finite six-direction realization is given by the proportions:

    p_R = (1 + u_x) / 6
    p_L = (1 - u_x) / 6
    p_U = (1 + u_y) / 6
    p_D = (1 - u_y) / 6
    p_F = (1 + u_z) / 6
    p_B = (1 - u_z) / 6.

Then:

    p_R - p_L = u_x / 3
    p_U - p_D = u_y / 3
    p_F - p_B = u_z / 3.

Therefore, after averaging over a complete instruction list:

    v = u / 3.

The speed is:

    |v| = 1/3,

independent of direction in the large-N limit. Finite lists introduce rounding
error, which vanishes as N grows.

This section is not required for the abstract combinatorial theorem. It is
required to show that a six-direction integer count model can approximate
ordinary three-dimensional Euclidean motion.

This is a direction-only construction at a fixed encoded speed. It should not be
confused with the more general velocity-imbalance dictionary:

    v = q/N.

The direction construction chooses proportions whose net speed is |v|=1/3 for
unit Euclidean directions. The general imbalance dictionary allows different
speed magnitudes, bounded only by the finite direction counts.


5. Exchange invariant
---------------------

The microscopic interaction rule is an add/remove exchange rule.

Allowed events:

    add(d):    N_d -> N_d + 1, N -> N + 1
    remove(d): N_d -> N_d - 1, N -> N - 1

for one direction d in:

    {R, L, U, D, F, B}.

Silent replacement is not allowed:

    replace(d1 -> d2) is forbidden as one event.

If a model needs replacement-like behavior, it must be represented as two
exchange events:

    remove(d1), then add(d2).

This matters because replacement would let the direction state change without
the energy/count accounting that gives the path its statistical cost.

In a closed system, one particle's removal may be another particle's addition,
or a carrier may transport the removed quantum. The total count of the closed
particle-plus-carrier system is conserved:

    N_total = constant.

In an open subsystem, a particle's N may change because it exchanges quanta with
carriers, sources, or surrounding matter.


6. Momentum, force, and directional exchange flux
-------------------------------------------------

The direction-count imbalance:

    q = (N_R - N_L, N_U - N_D, N_F - N_B)

plays the role of momentum up to a fixed scale. If one quantum carries momentum
unit p0, then:

    p = p0 q.

Since:

    v = q/N,

and mass/inertial content is proportional to N, this reproduces the structural
relation:

    p proportional to m v.

Now let lambda_d^+ be the add rate for direction d, and lambda_d^- the remove
rate for direction d. Then:

    d E[N_d] / dt = lambda_d^+ - lambda_d^-.

For the x component:

    d E[q_x] / dt =
        (lambda_R^+ - lambda_R^-)
      - (lambda_L^+ - lambda_L^-).

Similarly for y and z. In vector form:

    d E[q] / dt = sum_d e_d (lambda_d^+ - lambda_d^-),

where e_d is the primitive direction vector.

Thus force can be interpreted as the rate of biased momentum exchange:

    F proportional to dE[q]/dt.

If N is approximately constant over the interval, then:

    a = dv/dt ~= (1/N) dE[q]/dt.

Equivalently, force is directional exchange flux. Let:

    Phi_R, Phi_L, Phi_U, Phi_D, Phi_F, Phi_B

be the incoming or effective exchange-carrier fluxes in the six primitive
directions. The net directional flux is:

    Phi_vec =
        (Phi_R - Phi_L, Phi_U - Phi_D, Phi_F - Phi_B).

If the interaction converts this flux into add/remove changes of q with
efficiency eta, then:

    dE[q]/dt = eta Phi_vec.

If p = p0 q, then:

    F = dp/dt = p0 eta Phi_vec.

Therefore:

    force = biased exchange rate,
    force = directional exchange traffic,
    acceleration = normalized change of direction-count imbalance.

This section supplies the physical meaning of "external influence" in the
statistical path framework. A force is not a primitive command. It is the
macroscopic name for a flux of exchange carriers whose direction distribution
is not balanced.


7. Kinetic-like cost from balanced opposite directions
-----------------------------------------------------

To connect the statistical action to the classical action, one must recover a
quadratic velocity term.

The cleanest derivation begins in one dimension. At fixed total count N, let:

    N_R = N/2 + k
    N_L = N/2 - k.

The directional imbalance is:

    q = N_R - N_L = 2k.

The normalized velocity is:

    v = q/N = 2k/N.

The number of assignments with imbalance k is binomial:

    Omega(k) = N! / [(N/2+k)! (N/2-k)!].

This is maximal at k=0, the balanced rest-like state. Using Stirling expansion
for k much smaller than N:

    log Omega(k) ~= log Omega(0) - 2k^2/N.

Therefore the multiplicity loss, or statistical cost, is:

    -log[Omega(k)/Omega(0)] ~= 2k^2/N.

Substitute:

    k = vN/2.

Then:

    -log[Omega(v)/Omega(0)]
        ~= 2/N * (v^2 N^2 / 4)
        = (1/2) N v^2.

This is the important result:

    statistical cost of velocity imbalance ~= (1/2) N v^2.

Strictly speaking, this is a state-counting result. To use it as a path-action
term one must assume, or derive from the microscopic dynamics, that each
coarse-grained time slice contributes this local multiplicity penalty to the
history weight. Under that local-slice approximation:

    -log Prob(path) ~= sum_t local velocity-imbalance cost.

In the continuum notation this becomes an integral over time. Without this
extra step, the calculation only proves that velocity-imbalanced states have
lower multiplicity than balanced states.

If inertial mass is proportional to N, then this has the same velocity
dependence and the same 1/2 structure as kinetic energy:

    T = (1/2) m v^2.

In three dimensions, the same idea is applied pairwise to the opposite
directions:

    R/L, U/D, F/B.

For small imbalances:

    q = (N_R-N_L, N_U-N_D, N_F-N_B),
    v = q/N,

the total multiplicity penalty is quadratic in the vector velocity:

    -log[Omega(v)/Omega(0)] ~= constant * N |v|^2.

The exact coefficient depends on how the rest count is distributed among the
three opposite pairs and on the velocity normalization. The essential point is
not the coefficient. The essential point is that balanced rest mass gives a
quadratic log-multiplicity penalty for directional motion.


8. Energy accounting versus action cost
---------------------------------------

The statistical cost above is not physical energy by itself.

Physical energy accounting in this model is count accounting:

    E = epsilon N.

If a particle changes from:

    R = N/2, L = N/2

to:

    R = N/2 + k, L = N/2 - k,

then the total count N is unchanged. In that simplified closed-count example,
the total particle energy is unchanged by the redistribution. What changes is
the number of microscopic ways to realize the state. The moving state has lower
multiplicity than the balanced state.

Therefore there are two different quantities:

    physical energy/count       = epsilon N,
    statistical action cost     = -log Omega(path or state).

The bridge to kinetic energy is an interpretation: if inertial mass is
proportional to N, then the multiplicity penalty for directional imbalance has
the same quadratic form as kinetic energy. A complete physical model must still
specify how this statistical cost maps to measured energy units.

This distinction is important. The paper does not claim that:

    kinetic energy is literally -log Omega

without a conversion factor. It claims that the quadratic kinetic form can arise
from the multiplicity cost of moving away from balanced rest content.


9. Free Hamiltonian-like generator
----------------------------------

The previous section defined momentum-like imbalance:

    q = (N_R-N_L, N_U-N_D, N_F-N_B)

and velocity:

    v = dx/dt = q/N.

For a free particle with fixed N and no directional exchange flux:

    dq/dt = 0.

One may now ask whether there is a canonical-form-compatible generator
H_free(q) whose first canonical-form equation gives the count-velocity
relation:

    dx/dt = partial H_free / partial q.

Requiring:

    partial H_free / partial q = q/N

gives, by integration:

    H_free(q) = q^2/(2N) + constant.

Thus a free generator compatible with the canonical-form equation follows from
the count-velocity relation alone:

    H_free(q) = q^2/(2N).

Here H_free is a canonical-form-compatible generator, or statistical
kinetic-cost object, in the counting normalization. This does not by itself
derive the full symplectic structure of Hamiltonian mechanics. It is also not
yet the measured physical kinetic energy. To identify it with physical energy
one must supply the conversion between instruction count, momentum units, time
units, and ordinary energy units.

If physical momentum and mass are:

    p = p0 q,
    m proportional to N,

then this has the same structure as the ordinary kinetic Hamiltonian:

    H_free proportional to p^2/(2m).

This derivation does not require a force field. It is the generator of free
motion:

    dx/dt = q/N,
    dq/dt = 0.


10. Conservative flux and potential
-----------------------------------

The free generator does not describe a particle whose momentum changes. Momentum
changes require directional exchange flux:

    dq/dt proportional to F(x,t).

In this model, F is primary as a flux imbalance. A scalar potential exists only
when that force-like flux is conservative. Mathematically, this means:

    F = -grad V.

Equivalently, the line integral of F between two points is path-independent.
For such fields:

    integral F . dx = -Delta V.

Only under this condition can the flux contribution be represented by a scalar
potential V rather than by the full vector flux rule.

For example, the gravity-bridge note proposes a radial inverse-square
attractive flux of the form:

    F(r) = -K r_hat/r^2.

This flux is conservative away from the source and corresponds to:

    V(r) = -K/r.

By contrast, dissipative fluxes such as friction, or magnetic-like transverse
effects, generally cannot be represented by a single scalar potential.

The derivation of F is not universal. Each interaction must supply its own
exchange-flux rule. Gravity, electromagnetism, friction, and magnetic-like
effects correspond to different microscopic flux structures. In this division
of work, the gravity-bridge note is responsible for the source-directed 1/r^2
flux and local compensation assumptions; this least-action note only describes
how a conservative flux enters an action-like path description.


11. Lagrangian-like form under an additional condition
-----------------------------------------------------

The free-counting term gives a kinetic-like cost. A force or field requires a
position-dependent bias in exchange histories.

Suppose that, at position x, the microscopic exchange rules assign an additional
local weight:

    W(x, event).

Then a history h has probability weight:

    Prob(h) proportional to product_t W(x_t, event_t)

times its unbiased counting weight.

Taking negative logs gives:

    -log Prob(h) = counting cost - sum_t log W(x_t, event_t).

After coarse-graining, the second term can produce a flux-support contribution
to the effective path action:

    S_eff[path]
        = internal statistical cost - log(flux support).

If the flux is conservative:

    F = -grad V,

then classical mechanics permits a scalar potential description of the force.
However, this condition alone is not enough to derive the potential term from
microscopic probabilities. One must also show that the coarse-grained
log-support of exchange histories has the corresponding spatial dependence and
sign.

Therefore the following relation is a condition to be verified from the
microscopic rule, not a consequence of conservativity alone:

    -log(flux support) -> - beta V(x).

Section 12.2 proves that a precise local support-rate condition is sufficient
for this relation. Under that condition, the effective action can be written in
a Lagrangian-like form:

    S = integral (T - V) dt.

In the present notation, the corresponding schematic expression is:

    S_eff[path] ~= integral [ (1/2)N |v|^2 - beta V(x) ] dt,

where beta is a conversion coefficient between statistical cost and physical
units. The Euler-Lagrange equation for this expression gives:

    N d^2x/dt^2 = - beta grad V
                = beta F.

Thus, once an interaction-specific conservative flux has been supplied, and
once its exchange-history log-support has been shown to produce the potential
term with the required sign, the statistical kinetic term and the flux-derived
potential combine into the usual force law form.

Potential energy is therefore not fundamental in this model. It is a compact
macroscopic description of conservative directional exchange flux. The next
section states and proves sufficient bridge conditions rather than leaving this
step as an unspecified identification.


12. Two bridge lemmas
---------------------

12.1 Local-slice multiplicity lemma
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Let a coarse-grained path be divided into K slices of duration tau. Denote its
coarse state in slice j by z_j, including its velocity imbalance v_j. Assume:

1. the microscopic process is local in time at this resolution;
2. conditioned on the coarse boundary states, the microscopic choices inside
   different slices factorize; and
3. the number, or total base weight, of microscopic realizations of slice j is
   Omega(z_j).

Then the total weight of the coarse path is

    Prob[path] proportional to product_{j=0}^{K-1} Omega(z_j).

This is not an analogy. It follows directly from the multiplication rule for
independent conditional weights. Taking a negative logarithm proves

    S_eff[path]
        = -log Prob[path]
        = constant + sum_j [-log Omega(z_j)].

For the balanced opposite-direction counts derived in Section 7,

    -log[Omega(v_j)/Omega(0)]
        = (1/2) N |v_j|^2 + O(N |v_j|^4)

in the one-dimensional normalization, for |v_j| much smaller than 1. Therefore

    S_eff[path] - S_eff[rest]
        = sum_j (1/2) N |v_j|^2
          + sum_j O(N |v_j|^4).

Writing a sum as a Riemann integral gives

    sum_j (1/2) N |v_j|^2
        = integral [(N/(2 tau)) |v(t)|^2] dt.

The factor 1/tau converts dimensionless cost per slice into cost per unit time.
It may be absorbed into the conversion between statistical and physical units.
Thus the kinetic path cost is proved under the stated local-factorization
hypothesis. Without that hypothesis, state multiplicity alone does not imply a
path action.

The normalizations used in the bridge are:

    N:      dimensionless total instruction count of the particle;
    q:      dimensionless direction-count imbalance;
    v:      dimensionless coarse velocity, v = q/N;
    tau:    physical duration of one coarse time slice;
    beta:   conversion from field support units to statistical action units;
    N/tau:  inertial coefficient in the statistical equation of motion;
    m:      physical mass after choosing the count-to-mass/time conversion.

With these conventions, (1/2) N v^2 is a dimensionless per-slice statistical
cost, while (N/(2 tau)) v^2 is the corresponding cost rate in the continuum
notation. The measured kinetic energy requires an additional physical-unit
conversion.


12.2 Potential-support lemma
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Let A(x,Delta t) be the total multiplicative support supplied by local exchange
events while a path occupies position x for a short interval Delta t. Assume
that, after summing over the allowed add/remove events in that interval, its
position-dependent part satisfies

    A(x,Delta t) = A_0(Delta t)
                   exp[beta V(x) Delta t + o(Delta t)].

Equivalently,

    lim_{Delta t -> 0}
        {log A(x,Delta t) - log A_0(Delta t)}/Delta t
        = beta V(x).

For a path sampled at positions x_j, locality gives

    A[path] = product_j A(x_j,Delta t).

Therefore

    -log A[path]
        = position-independent constant
          - beta sum_j V(x_j) Delta t
          + sum_j o(Delta t).

Taking the continuum limit proves

    -log A[path]
        = constant - beta integral V(x(t)) dt.

Hence the required minus sign and the time integral of V are consequences of
the stated microscopic support law; they are not inserted after the fact.
Combining this result with the local-slice lemma gives

    S_eff[path]
        = constant
          + integral [(N/(2 tau)) |v|^2 - beta V(x)] dt

to quadratic order in velocity.

If the macroscopic directional exchange flux is conservative,

    F = -grad V,

then every smooth interior minimum of the statistical functional is stationary.
On time intervals for which the corresponding classical extremal is a local
minimum, the Euler-Lagrange equation gives

    (N/tau) a = beta F.

After defining physical mass by m = N/tau and defining the physical force as
F_phys = beta F, this is

    m a = F_phys.

The lemma is a sufficient-condition theorem, not a claim that every add/remove
process automatically has this support law. This is the main field-specific
verification burden of the paper. A microscopic model of a concrete field must
calculate A(x,Delta t) and verify its exponential rate; otherwise the potential
term has merely been assumed at the support/probability level. In this sense,
the potential-support law is almost the potential term itself written in
exponential microscopic-support form. The useful content of the lemma is not
that it magically derives V; it states exactly what a field model must verify
for V to enter the statistical action. Directional local detailed balance,

    log[W(x -> x+dx)/W(x+dx -> x)]
        = beta F(x) . dx
        = -beta [V(x+dx)-V(x)] + o(|dx|),

is enough to identify the conservative drift and its potential difference, but
by itself it produces an endpoint ratio. The occupation-support condition above
is the additional condition that produces the classical time integral
integral V dt.

The restriction to a local minimum matters. A positive probability cost always
selects minima, whereas Hamilton's principle more generally selects stationary
paths, which may become saddles on long intervals. The lemma therefore proves
the local least-action bridge; it does not turn an arbitrary classical saddle
into a probability maximum.

From this point onward, the worked examples use the stationary Euler-Lagrange
equation of the effective functional. The probability interpretation justifies
that stationary path as a most-supported path only in local-minimum regimes and
over intervals short enough to avoid saddle-path complications.


13. Worked example: constant force
----------------------------------

The cleanest non-free example is a constant directional exchange flux.

Suppose the net exchange flux produces a constant force-like term in one
direction:

    F = constant.

In one dimension this is automatically conservative, because it can be written
as:

    F = -dV/dx.

Choose:

    V(x) = -F x.

Then:

    -dV/dx = F.

For constant force, the support law can be motivated without first assuming an
arbitrary potential shape. A uniform directional exchange bias gives the same
position-dependent support rate at every point. Moving the path endpoint by dx
in the force direction changes the accumulated local support by a constant
log-factor proportional to dx:

    d log A / dx = -beta F Delta t.

The sign can look counterintuitive. For positive F, this expression says that
the local occupation support decreases as x increases in the force direction.
That is acceptable here because A(x,Delta t) is not a rule saying "more local
support where the particle is pushed." It is the occupation-support factor that
produces the potential contribution to a stationary path functional. With the
negative-log convention, this sign is exactly what gives the Lagrangian term
-V(x) = +F x and therefore the equation N a = F.

Integrating over position gives:

    log A(x,Delta t) = log A_0(Delta t) - beta F x Delta t + o(Delta t).

Since V(x) = -F x, this is:

    A(x,Delta t) = A_0(Delta t) exp[beta V(x) Delta t + o(Delta t)].

Thus the constant-force case supplies a concrete realization of the
potential-support lemma: constant exchange bias gives a linear potential.

Using this support law:

    -log(flux support) -> - beta V(x),

then the Lagrangian-like expression becomes:

    L = (1/2) N v^2 - V(x)
      = (1/2) N v^2 + F x.

The Euler-Lagrange equation gives:

    d/dt (partial L / partial v) = partial L / partial x,

so:

    d/dt (N v) = F,

or:

    N a = F.

Thus a constant biased exchange flux reproduces the usual constant-acceleration
law once the statistical kinetic term has been identified and the
flux-support-to-potential calibration has been supplied.

This is the minimal complete case:

    discrete histories -> kinetic statistical cost,
    constant directional flux -> constant force,
    conservative flux plus the support lemma -> linear potential,
    stationary effective action -> N a = F.


14. Worked example: radial gravity-like flux
--------------------------------------------

Gravity should enter this note only after a separate interaction model has
supplied a mean conservative flux.

The gravity-bridge note proposes such a first-approximation flux. In that
picture:

    source traffic in 3D        -> event density proportional to M/r^2,
    discrete direction coding   -> mean vector direction r_hat,
    add/remove exchange drift   -> acceleration along the source direction,
    local channel compensation  -> approximate cancellation of target count.

If that separate bridge supplies the local Newtonian-looking acceleration:

    a = -G_eff M r_hat/r^2,

then the force on a target constituent of effective inertial count N is:

    F(r) = N a
         = -N G_eff M r_hat/r^2.

This field is conservative away from the source. Its potential is:

    V(r) = -N G_eff M/r.

The Lagrangian-like expression is then:

    L = (1/2) N |v|^2 - V(r)
      = (1/2) N |v|^2 + N G_eff M/r.

The Euler-Lagrange equation gives:

    N a = -N G_eff M r_hat/r^2,

and therefore:

    a = -G_eff M r_hat/r^2.

This example is deliberately formal. It shows how a conservative gravity-like
flux would be represented once supplied. It does not derive the 1/r^2 source
flux, the discrete direction rule, the add/remove drift coefficient, or the
local compensation law. Those belong to the gravity-bridge paper.


15. Relation to classical least action
-------------------------------------

Classical mechanics usually introduces the action:

    S_cl[path] = integral L dt,

with:

    L = T - V,

and then requires:

    delta S_cl = 0.

This paper does not start by postulating that functional. It starts from path
probabilities:

    S_eff[path] = -log Prob(path).

The connection to classical mechanics requires the following identifications:

1. The log-multiplicity penalty for velocity imbalance supplies the kinetic
   term:

       -log Omega(v) ~= (1/2) N |v|^2
                     ~= const * (1/2) m |v|^2.

2. Directional exchange flux supplies the force term:

       F = dp/dt.

3. If, and only if, that flux is conservative, it can be represented by a scalar
   potential:

       F = -grad V.

4. The potential-like term must come from the negative log of conservative
   local exchange support:

       potential-like term = negative log of local exchange support.

This fourth step is conditional. A conservative macroscopic force permits a
potential V, but the microscopic model must still show that the exchange-history
weights produce the required -V contribution to the action.

Under those conditions, the statistically dominant path can play a role
analogous to a stationary-action path:

    most probable path = minimum/effective-stationary S_eff path.

The result is not a derivation of every possible classical field. It is a
conditional bridge: the statistical kinetic core is field-independent, while
the force/potential term is supplied by a separate interaction-specific flux
model.


16. What is required and what is optional
----------------------------------------

Required for the combinatorial action layer:

1. microscopic histories;
2. macroscopic paths as coarse-grained classes of histories;
3. probabilities or weights of histories;
4. definition S_eff = -log Prob(path).

Required for the discrete-direction physical realization:

1. a finite discrete direction basis;
2. nonnegative integer counts in each direction;
3. local one-quantum add/remove events;
4. no silent replacement;
5. total count/energy accounting in closed systems;
6. a physical dictionary from counts to momentum, velocity, and inertial
   content.

The exact six-direction axial basis is not required for the abstract
combinatorial theorem, but it is useful for physical interpretation because it
makes direction counting finite, integer-valued, and compatible with ordinary
three-dimensional Euclidean motion through direction proportions.

External forces are not required for the abstract theorem or for the free
Hamiltonian-like generator. They enter when one wants the effective action to
describe physical motion in an environment. In that case the force must be
given as directional exchange flux. A scalar potential can be introduced only
for conservative fluxes satisfying F = -grad V.


17. What is not derived here
----------------------------

This note does not derive:

1. the exact numerical coefficient of classical kinetic energy;
2. the exact textbook Hamilton action for all systems;
3. quantum phases or the Feynman path integral;
4. relativistic action;
5. gauge fields;
6. the inverse-square gravity-like source flux, which belongs to the separate
   gravity-bridge model;
7. the detailed microscopic source of position-dependent exchange bias;
8. the force/flux rules for all fields;
9. the local compensation law required for target-independent gravity, which is
   also part of the gravity-bridge model;
10. that every physical interaction satisfies the local-slice factorization
    hypothesis of Section 12.1;
11. that a specified microscopic gravity, electromagnetic, or other field model
    satisfies the occupation-support law of Section 12.2.

Items 10 and 11 are now precise model-verification obligations rather than
missing algebraic steps. Section 12 proves what follows if their explicit
hypotheses hold. What remains is to derive those hypotheses from the concrete
add/remove rules of each proposed interaction.

It only derives the structure:

    path probability -> effective action,
    direction-count imbalance -> momentum/velocity,
    multiplicity penalty -> kinetic-like quadratic term,
    free Hamiltonian generator -> H_free = q^2/(2N),
    exchange flux -> force,
    local factorization -> time-additive kinetic path cost,
    potential-support law -> potential term with the required sign,
    conservative exchange flux -> force from that potential.


18. Conclusion
--------------

The local principle of least-action-like path selection can be interpreted as a
statistical selection law.
At the most general level:

    S_eff[path] = -log Prob(path),

so the most probable macroscopic path is the path of least effective action.
This statement refers to support-maximizing regimes. Classical Hamilton's
principle is more general: on long intervals it can select stationary saddle
paths, not only probability maxima.

In a six-direction discrete exchange model, a particle's motion state is encoded
by integer counts of primitive momentum directions. Local interactions add or
remove one quantum; they do not silently replace one direction with another.
The normalized count imbalance gives velocity, while the imbalance itself gives
momentum-like content.

At fixed total count, binomial/multinomial counting penalizes directional
imbalance quadratically. In the simplest balanced one-dimensional pair:

    -log[Omega(v)/Omega(0)] ~= (1/2) N v^2.

If mass is proportional to total count, this has the same form as a kinetic
term. The same count-velocity relation gives a free Hamiltonian-like generator:

    H_free(q) = q^2/(2N).

External forces are introduced as directional exchange fluxes. If a particular
interaction supplies a conservative flux:

    F = -grad V,

then that flux can be represented by a potential term and combined with the
statistical kinetic term in a Lagrangian-like expression. The flux itself is not
universal; it must be derived separately for each interaction.

Thus a local least-action-like law may emerge from discrete momentum-exchange
histories:

    action is the statistical cost of a macroscopic path,
    and, in the local-minimum regime, the classical extremal is the path with
    the greatest microscopic support.
